\chapter{Conclusion} \label{cha:conclusion}

This thesis investigated whether detokenization in large language models depends on a set of specialized attention heads, and how this process is shaped by tokenizer choice, language, and model size. Across most tested settings, targeted ablation of the highest scoring attention heads caused larger degradation in word retrieval than random ablation, particularly at higher ablation levels. In several cases, especially for larger models, retrieval performance collapsed under targeted ablation while remaining comparatively stable under random ablation. These findings suggest that detokenization is not purely a distributed or redundant process, but instead relies disproportionately on a relatively small subset of attention heads involved in forming intermediary word-level representations from fragmented token inputs.

The findings also show that this process is not uniform across model configurations. Tokenizer choice affects both retrieval performance and robustness to ablation, but not in a way that can be explained by compression efficiency or vocabulary size alone. Instead, the results indicate that tokenization influences how lexical information is internally structured, with some schemes encouraging more localized specialization and others more distributed representations. Model size also shapes these patterns. Larger models generally achieve stronger retrieval performance and exhibit sharper early-layer retrieval peaks, suggesting that scaling is associated with earlier and more specialized attention heads.

These findings support the broader hypothesis that detokenization is an identifiable process in decoder-only language models. At the same time, they suggest that the internal realization of this stage depends on tokenizer design and model scale. This has implications both for mechanistic interpretability, where conclusions may not transfer cleanly across architectures and tokenizers, and for model design, where tokenization choices may shape not only efficiency but also the internal organization of representations.

Future work should examine whether these detokenization-related heads are equally important for generation, and how such mechanisms emerge over the course of training.
