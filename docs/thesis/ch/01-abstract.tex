\chapter*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}
\pagestyle{plain}

Large language models process text as sequences of tokens, raising the question of how coherent full representations are formed out of fragmented words during inference. Prior work has hypothesized a \textit{detokenization} stage in early layers, where tokens are recombined into larger units, but direct intervention-based evidence for the role of specific components remains limited. This thesis investigates whether a 1/3/5\% subset of attention heads plays a role in this process. Candidate heads are identified based on their preference for within-word attention (based on attention weight and $L_2$ norm) and their contribution is tested using targeted ablation and activation patching in a word reconstruction task. Across languages (English, Turkish, Chinese), tokenization schemes (BPE, SentencePiece, byte-level), and model families, results show that ablating these heads in most cases leads to degradation in reconstruction performance exceeding random ablation. As parameter size increases so does specialization, with steeper and taller early peaks in retrieval performance. The results provide additional evidence for the detokenization hypothesis and suggest that early layers rely on specialized attention heads to construct internal word-level representations from fragmented inputs.
