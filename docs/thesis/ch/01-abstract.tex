\chapter*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}
\pagestyle{plain}

Large language models process text as sequences of subtokens, raising the question of how coherent full representations are formed out of fragmented subtokens during inference. Prior work has hypothesized a \textit{detokenization} stage in early layers, where subtokens are recombined into larger units, but direct intervention-based evidence for the role of specific components remains limited. We investigate whether a 5\% subset of attention heads plays a role in this process. We identify candidate heads based on their preference for within-word attention (based on attention weight and $L_2$ norm) and test their contribution using targeted ablation and activation patching in a word reconstruction task. Across languages (English, Turkish, Chinese), tokenization schemes (BPE, SentencePiece, byte-level), and model families, we find that ablating these heads leads to degradation in reconstruction performance exceeding random ablation. As parameter size increases so does specialization, with steeper and taller early peaks in retrieval performance. Our results provide additional evidence for the detokenization hypothesis and suggest that early transformer layers rely on specialized attention heads to construct internal word-level representations from fragmented inputs.
