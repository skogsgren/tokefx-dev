Large language models (LLMs) are trained on vast amounts of text using a next-token prediction objective, yet exhibit strong performance across a wide range of language tasks \citep{brown2020}. Despite this empirical success, our understanding of how these models perform such generalization remains limited \citep{sharkey2025}. One aspect of this problem is how textual input is represented and processed within the model.

Rather than operating on words directly, LLMs rely on tokenization schemes that segment text into a fixed vocabulary of units, allowing them to handle input without an impractically large word-level lexicon. These tokens often correspond to subword fragments. For example, the city name \textit{Paterson} is represented in the GPT-2 tokenizer as \texttt{['Pat', 'erson']}. Because all subsequent computation operates on these tokens, any information that spans multiple tokens must be reconstructed during inference. Understanding this reconstruction is therefore important for explaining the disproportionate impact of tokenization across languages \citep{petrov2023, ahia2023}, as well as model robustness to typos \citep{zhao2026evaluatingrobustnesslargelanguage, cao2023} and non-canonical segmentation \citep{gutierrez2023, zheng2026}. This requires examining how tokenization schemes shape model input, how representations are transformed within the model, and how prior work has analyzed internal computations in LLMs.

This chapter is structured as follows: Section~\ref{sec:background_tokenization} introduces common tokenization algorithms, Section~\ref{sec:background_transformer} outlines the relevant components of the transformer architecture, and Section~\ref{sec:background_interpretability} presents mechanistic interpretability as a field, and then finally Section~\ref{sec:background_detokenization} going into detail on the \textit{detokenization hypothesis}, which proposes that early layers reconstruct larger linguistic units from fragmented inputs.

\section{Tokenization in LLMs}\label{sec:background_tokenization}

Compression-based subword tokenization algorithms are the standard for modern LLMs \citep[Chapter 2]{jm3}. The primary motivation for subword tokenization is to shrink the vocabulary to a manageable size and to mitigate the out-of-vocabulary problems of earlier word-based approaches \citep{mielke2021}. Despite their arbitrary nature, tokens have been shown to reflect semantics \citep{haslett2025semantics} and psychological saliency \citep{haslett2025psychological}. In practice, several related approaches are used to construct such subword vocabularies, differing primarily in how they segment text and assign vocabulary candidates. The most widely used methods include \textbf{Byte Pair Encoding (BPE)}, \textbf{Unigram} language model tokenization, and \textbf{byte-level} tokenization. These approaches represent different trade-offs.

\begin{description}

\item[Byte-Pair Encoding]
Byte-Pair Encoding (BPE), introduced by \citet{bpe2} and adapted for neural machine translation by \citet{bpe}, constructs a subword vocabulary by iteratively merging frequent symbol/byte pairs. In practice, BPE tokenization involves a \textit{pre-tokenization} step followed by a merge learning procedure. Pre-tokenization defines the initial units over which merges are applied, often using simple heuristics such as splitting on whitespace and punctuation \citep{schmidt2025}. For insufficiently trained byte-based BPE tokenizers, we can end up with \textit{glitch} tokens \citep{land2024} due to how languages such as Chinese is represented in bytes, where each character is $\sim$ 3 bytes, and where many of those bytes only correspond to something meaningful when combined in the right order.

\item[Unigram]
Unigram tokenization \citep{kudo2018} models tokenization as a probabilistic process to provide subword regularization, selecting segmentations based on a learned distribution over possible subword units using a Unigram model. Unlike BPE, it does not rely on deterministic merging rules and can represent multiple valid segmentations. This flexibility is particularly useful in multilingual settings, where assumptions about token boundaries (e.g., whitespace) may not hold. Implementations such as SentencePiece \citep{sentencepiece} avoid explicit pre-tokenization, enabling more language-agnostic processing by not making assumptions what a token \textit{can} be beforehand.

\item[Byte-level Tokenization]
Some approaches, such as ByT5 \citep{xue2022}, operate directly on raw byte sequences without applying compression. This reduces preprocessing assumptions and can improve robustness to unseen inputs in some cases \citep{aleph2026}, but results in longer input sequences and more fragmented sequences.

\end{description}

\citet{arnett2025, wegmann2025} show that pre-tokenization choices can disproportionately affect non-Latin languages, leading to suboptimal segmentations and degraded performance in multilingual settings. More generally, tokenization and model design in LLMs are often English-centric \citep{joshi2020, blasi2022}, resulting in lower performance and higher computational costs for other languages \citep{ahia2023, petrov2023}.

\section{Transformer Architecture}\label{sec:background_transformer}

Most modern large language models are autoregressive decoder-only models based on the transformer architecture introduced by \citet{vaswani2017}. At a high level, a decoder-only transformer processes a sequence of token representations and builds contextualized embeddings through a series of layered transformations. In each layer, token representations are gradually updated by incorporating information from other tokens in the sequence. The resulting representations are then used to predict the next token in the sequence.

Before these transformations can take place, the discrete tokens produced by the tokenizer must be converted into vector representations that the model can process. This is done using an input embedding layer, which maps each token in the vocabulary to a dense vector. Since the transformer architecture does not inherently encode sequence order, positional information is added to these embeddings to allow the model to distinguish between different token positions in the input. Modern LLMs, like Llama \citep{llama2} and Qwen \citep{qwen2, qwen2.5} tend to utilize RoPE \citep{rope} where instead of adding absolute position embeddings we \textit{rotate} its input by an angle. This results in the model encoding \textit{relative} position differences rather than absolute position differences, which has been shown to lead to specialized syntactic attention heads \citep{barbero2025}. The resulting representations (token embeddings + positional encodings) are then passed through a stack of transformer blocks, which iteratively refine the representations. After the final layer, a normalization step and a linear projection map the representations to the vocabulary space, producing a probability distribution over possible next tokens. Transformer blocks consist of a self-attention layer and a feed-forward network, each wrapped with residual connections and layer normalization, visualized in Figure~\ref{fig:transformer_block}. The central concept in the transformer block is the self-attention layer. In a self-attention layer, three linear projections of an input sequence with dimensionality $d$ produces the \textit{Query}, \textit{Key}, and \textit{Value} vectors. These vectors $Q$, $K$, and $V$ are then used to calculate attention scores:

\begin{equation}\label{eq:attention}
\text{Att}(Q, K, V) = \text{softmax} \left( \frac{QK^{\top}}{\sqrt{d}} \right) V
\end{equation}

\noindent
In decoder-only transformers, training is performed using next-token prediction, where attention scores for tokens occurring after the current token are masked. To allow the model to capture different types of relationships in parallel, transformers use \textit{multi-head attention}. This involves applying Equation \ref{eq:attention} many times in parallel, forming several \textit{attention heads} which can attend to distinct parts of the sequence. Each head is calculated as such, where $W_i$ represents the learnable weight matrices for the $i$-th attention head:

\begin{equation}\label{eq:multihead}
\text{head}_i (Q, K, V) = \text{Att}(QW_i^{Q}, KW_i^{K}, VW_i^{V})
\end{equation}

Modern large language models such as LLaMA2 \citep{llama2} and Qwen \citep{qwen2.5,qwen2} follow the transformer architecture with several modifications to improve efficiency and training stability. Rather than describing the original transformer in detail, this work focuses on the specific design choices used in these models since they are subsequently used for analysis.

In the attention mechanism, both model families employ Grouped Query Attention (GQA) \citep{ainslie2023}, where multiple query heads share keys and values. This reduces memory bandwidth and improves inference speed, at the potential cost of increased interactions between heads compared to standard multi-head attention.

The feed-forward component uses a gated formulation based on SwiGLU \citep{swiglu}, instead of the standard two-layer ReLU network. SwiGLU combines a Swish-activated linear transformation with another linear transformation through element-wise multiplication. The Swish activation is defined as
\begin{equation}
Swish(x) = \frac{x}{1 + e^{-x}}
\end{equation}
Given input $x$ and weight matrices $W_1, W_2$, the feed-forward transformation becomes
\begin{equation}
\text{SwiGLU}(x) = Swish(xW_1) \odot xW_2
\end{equation}
This formulation makes gradients smooth, as compared to RELU which has a hard cutoff between positive and negative input values.

Layer normalization is performed using RMSNorm \citep{rmsnorm}, which scales activations without mean-centering, unlike the default LayerNorm. It is defined as
\begin{equation}
y_i = \frac{x_i}{\mathrm{RMS}(x)} \gamma_i, \quad
\mathrm{RMS}(x) = \sqrt{\epsilon + \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i^2}
\end{equation}
RMSNorm improves training efficiency and is shown to have neglible negative effects in large models, though it may be less stable in smaller-scale settings.

\section{Mechanistic Interpretability}\label{sec:background_interpretability}

While the mathematical operations and components of neural networks, including transformers, are well understood, explaining how specific outputs emerge remains a challenge\footnote{When talking activation functions, \citet[3]{swiglu} even goes as far as to state \textit{``We offer no explanation as to why these architectures seem to work; we attribute their success, as all else, to divine benevolence.''} Who knew activation functions could be so theological?}. This has motivated the field of mechanistic interpretability, which aims to understand how model behavior emerges by reverse engineering the computations. Early efforts were mostly concerned with vision \citep{cammarata2020a}, and focused on identifying visually interpretable features. For example, features were found in neural models corresponding to grocery store detectors \citep{nguyen2016}, curve detectors \citep{cammarata2020b}, or even multi-modal features \citep{goh2021}.  However, extending these approaches to language directly is challenging as the representations are less directly interpretable\footnote{\citet{elhage2022a, templeton2024, bricken2023} argues this is because many neurons in LLMs are in \textit{super-position}, meaning that one neuron's direction in feature space corresponds to many separate features. A detailed discussion of this phenomenon is beyond the scope of this thesis.}, though some higher-level features have been shown to be linearly separable \citep{park2024}.

One approach is to analyze model behavior in terms of interacting components, often described as \textit{circuits}, that contribute to specific outputs. As argued by \citet{elhage2021}, all components of the transformer can be viewed as participating in such circuits through their interactions via the \textit{residual stream}. The residual stream acts as a ``communication channel'': each transformer block receives its input from it, and both the attention and feed-forward network write their output back into it. It is shown in red in Figure~\ref{fig:transformer_block}. The result is an additive process, where the model's representation is built up incrementally as it moves through the network.

Within this framework, attention heads provide a useful unit of analysis. Because each head computes attention patterns independently, heads can become functionally specialized. Prior work has identified heads in BERT \citep{devlin2019}, an encoder-only transformer, associated with syntactic structure \citep{clark2019}. \citet{liu2025} identified heads in LLMs corresponding to specific languages, and were able to steer the model towards specific languages by adjusting their activations. There's also more studies on more task-specific behaviors such as relational reasoning \citep{hanna2023} and acronym detection \citep{carrasco2024}. At the same time, many heads can be removed with limited impact on performance \citep{voita2019, michel2019}, suggesting that only a subset contribute to specific computations. \citet{zhou2025} show that ablating even a single head in LLaMA2-7B leads to a 16x increase in harmful responses.

This specialization appears to vary systematically across layers. \citet{lad2024} proposes that transformer models exhibit \textit{stage-wise} processing, where different layers perform distinct types of computation: early layers perform \textit{\textbf{detokenization}}, where subtokens merge into larger lexical units; middle layers perform \textit{feature engineering}, which constructs intermediate semantic features; \textit{prediction ensembling} in middle-late layers, where candidate next-token predictions emerge; and in the final layers \textit{residual sharpening}, which concentrates probability mass onto a small set of tokens. Empirical evidence supports this layered organization: syntactic information has been shown to be most prominent in intermediate layers \citep{vig2019}, while more task-specific or output-oriented representations emerge later \citep{ferrando2024similarity}. Similar patterns have also been observed in multilingual models, where representations shift between language-specific and more shared structures across layers \citep{conneau2020, wendler2024}. This hierarchical organization is also consistent with patterns observed in other domains. In computer vision, early convolutional layers detect simple features such as edges, while deeper layers capture increasingly complex structures \citep{zeiler2014, jastrzebski2018}. Similar hierarchical processing has also been hypothesized in the human brain \citep{wacongne2011}.

\section{Detokenization}\label{sec:background_detokenization}

Evidence for detokenization as an early-stage process comes from studies of subtoken merging behavior. \citet{elhage2021} demonstrate in toy transformer models that attention heads can implement skip-trigram-like mappings, effectively reconstructing token sequences from fragmented inputs. For example, a tokenized sequence of ``Cindy'' without a space \texttt{["C", "indy"]} can be mapped back to the more typical form \texttt{["␣Cindy"]} through specific attention head patterns. Related work has also identified neurons in early layers that respond strongly to token boundary artifacts \citep{elhage2022a}, suggesting that models encode information about subtoken structure at early stages.

\citet{kaplan2025} argue that detokenization is a two-stage process, in which attention mechanisms first identify relevant subtoken information and feed-forward networks (FFNs) subsequently map it into an implicit internal vocabulary. This view builds on \citet{feucht2024}, who show that representations of multi-token entities become increasingly abstract across layers, with token-level information concentrated early. Within this framework, feed-forward networks act as key/value memories \citep{geva2021, geva2022, meng2022} that integrate information selected by attention. \citet{kaplan2025} support this account empirically, showing that attention is stronger within multi-token words and that ablating FFN outputs degrades performance more than ablating hidden states.

Prior work identifies attention heads associated with both subtoken merging and higher-level semantic copying, but largely does not establish whether these heads are necessary for forming coherent internal representations or whether later components can compensate. For example, \citet{feucht2025} study ``concept attention heads'' that copy semantic units in tasks such as translation and synonymy, while other work identifies heads involved in subtoken-level structure, such as attending within word boundaries or merging subtokens \citep{ferrando2024informationflow, lad2024, kamoda2025}. However, the causal importance of these heads, and whether their roles generalize across tokenizers, languages, or model scales, remains unclear. This work addresses these gaps by testing whether a set of attention heads is critical for forming internal representations, and how their importance varies with tokenization and language.
