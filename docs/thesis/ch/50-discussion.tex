\chapter{Discussion} \label{cha:discussion}

This chapter begins with a summary of the main findings in relation to the research questions. Section~\ref{sec:discussion_tokenizer} discusses the effects of tokenizer choice, while Section~\ref{sec:discussion_qwen} focuses on model size, followed by a reflection on limitations and implications in Section~\ref{sec:discussion_limitations}, and directions for future work in Section~\ref{sec:discussion_futurework}.

\begin{description}
\item[\textbf{RQ1}] The results show targeted ablation has a disproportionately large impact on word retrieval compared to random ablation in most cases, especially at larger ablation levels. In most cases, models are unable to recover retrieval performance after targeted ablation, and retrieval peaks occur later in the network. In some cases, such as the \texttt{qwen2.5} 7B model for 3/5\% ablation, retrieval collapses from 0.79 to 0.08/0.01, while random ablation reduces performance by 0.06/0.18 respectively. This pattern is consistent with findings by \citet{voita2019}, indicating that a small subset of attention heads plays a specialized role.

\item[\textbf{RQ2}] Tokenizer choice affects the formation of intermediate representations. Differences in retrieval performance cannot be explained by compression efficiency alone. For example, the \texttt{xglm} tokenizer achieves higher retrieval than \texttt{gpt2} in English (0.81 vs.\ 0.47), despite \texttt{gpt2} being better at compressing English. Vocabulary size also fails to explain the difference, seeing as the \texttt{aya} tokenizer has a similar vocabulary size, but has \textit{worse} retrieval than \texttt{gpt2}. This suggests that tokenizer design influences how the representations are structured within the model during training. At the same time, robustness to ablation does not follow a consistent pattern across tokenizers, and varies depending on language.

\item[\textbf{RQ3}] Larger models achieve higher retrieval scores, with \texttt{qwen2.5} 7B performing best overall and 0.5B performing worst. Mid-sized models (1.5B and 3B) show similar performance, albeit with different patterns. Larger models show different retrieve rate patterns, with a distinct peak at early layers which is not present in the 1.5B and 3B models, that instead show a more distributed retrieval pattern. This suggests that scaling is associated with more localized or specialized components for whole word reconstruction. 1\% ablation has smallest impact, 3/5\% more --- with 3\% ablation closely matching the detrimental impact of 5\% in the majority of cases. Similarly to \textbf{RQ2} there are no clear patterns in robustness to ablation which apply to all languages and parameter sizes.

\end{description}

\section{Tokenizer Impact}\label{sec:discussion_tokenizer}

The impact of tokenizer choice can be partly explained by differences in compression and the resulting training dataset size in Toksuite models. Because all models are trained on a fixed number of tokens, tokenizers with lower compression rates expose the model to less raw text. As shown in Table~\ref{tab:fertility}, \texttt{byt5} operates on 1~byte per token, meaning that it is effectively trained on less data than more efficient tokenizers such as \texttt{xglm} which gets $\sim$4~bytes per token. This reduced exposure may limit the model's ability to learn specialized attention patterns. Instead of relying on a small set of heads to reconstruct word-level representations, the model may develop more distributed mechanisms for combining token information, reducing the impact of targeted ablation.

However, compression alone does not explain the observed differences. For example, the \texttt{gpt2} tokenizer achieves a compression rate close to \texttt{byt5} for Chinese (1.36 vs.\ 1.0), yet attains higher retrieval performance (0.36 vs.\ 0.12). Similarly, the \texttt{xglm} tokenizer outperforms \texttt{gpt2} in English by almost double (0.81 vs.\ 0.47) despite slightly worse compression. Vocabulary size is also insufficient to explain the gap: \texttt{aya} and \texttt{xglm} have nearly identical vocabularies (256k vs.\ 255k) but exhibit different retrieval rates. These comparisons indicate that factors beyond compression, training dataset size, and vocabulary size plays a role.

Cross-lingual transfer may be one such factor. Prior work shows that monolingual BERT models can learn abstractions that generalize across languages \citep{artetxe2020}, and that embedding alignment correlates with transfer \citep{deshpande2022}. The \texttt{gpt2}-based model may therefore benefit from representations learned from other languages when processing Chinese/Turkish, helping compensate for less efficient tokenization. In contrast, the explicitly multilingual \texttt{aya} tokenizer performs worse than \texttt{gpt2} in both English and Turkish despite more efficient tokenization. This is consistent with the ``curse of multilinguality'' \citep{chang2024}: given fixed model capacity, increasing the number of languages can \textit{reduce} per-language performance due to competition for shared representational resources. This apparent contradiction, that the less efficient \texttt{gpt2} tokenizer nevertheless yields stronger robustness and performance than explicitly multilingual tokenizers, may reflect the benefits of vocabulary overlap identified by \cite{kallini2025}, who show that partially shared vocabularies can improve cross-lingual transfer relative to fully disjoint vocabularies, even in the presence of multilingual capacity trade-offs.

Tokenizer efficiency may also influence how representations are distributed. Poor tokenization may force the model to distribute computation across more components. In contrast, better tokenization may concentrate this functionality in a smaller set of specialized heads. This interpretation is supported by attention heatmaps for Chinese (Figure~\ref{fig:gpt2_heatmaps}), which show weaker specialization than in English and Turkish. Unicode artifacts such as the ``ghost token'' effect \citep{jang2025} may further disrupt representation formation or make them more distributed, particularly in languages where such artifacts are frequent (like Chinese), aligning with prior findings on the importance of pre-tokenization \citep{wegmann2025, arnett2025}.

Tokenizer architecture itself may also play a role. The \texttt{xglm} tokenizer achieves the highest retrieval performance in English, outperforming even larger models such as 7B \texttt{qwen2.5}, despite slightly worse compression than \texttt{gpt2}. This suggests that differences in tokenization strategy (e.g., Unigram vs.\ BPE) may influence how effectively embeddings and intermediate representations are learned. While prior work emphasizes the dominant role of the embedding layer as compared to the tokenizer itself \citep{wu2023}, these results indicate that tokenizer design can still shape how efficiently embeddings are learned during training.

\section{Parameter Size Impact}\label{sec:discussion_qwen}

Retrieval performance generally increases with parameter size, with the 0.5B model performing worst and the 7B model performing best within the \texttt{qwen2.5} family. This is consistent with prior findings regarding how embeddings emerge during training: embeddings of high-frequency words converge quickly during training, while low-frequency words take longer and retain alignment with the bias in their random initializations \citep{papadimitriou2025}. However, this relationship does not strictly hold in the results in comparison to parameter size. The 1.5B model slightly outperforms the 3B model in English and Chinese. This suggests that increasing parameter size does not uniformly improve representation quality across configurations. One interpretation is that scaling changes \textit{how} representations are constructed within the model, where smaller models may rely on more distributed mechanisms, while larger models develop specialized components.

This shift is reflected in the layerwise retrieval patterns (Figure~\ref{fig:qwen_rr}). The 7B and 3B models exhibit qualitatively distinct early peaks (within the first 25\% of layers), followed by a rapid decline, suggesting key representations are handled by specific components early, and then disregarded. In contrast, the 0.5B and 1.5B models show more gradual and distributed accumulation of retrieval across layers. Similar patterns have been observed in larger models \citep{kaplan2025}, as well as in studies showing that structured behaviors such as induction are less clearly localized in smaller models \citep{feucht2025}.

Within this framework, the 1.5B model may represent an intermediate regime in which layer specialization is emerging but not yet fully established. This could explain its performance relative to the 3B model despite more distributed behavior across layers, and is consistent with phase transition phenomena observed during training, where models abruptly shift toward more specialized structure after sufficient training \citep{elhage2022b,power2022}.

\section{Limitations}\label{sec:discussion_limitations}

The primary limitation concerns the candidate-selection used to identify attention heads for ablation using boundary conditions. Because candidate heads are selected from token patterns, the resulting heads may include components that are correlated with detokenization but not exclusively specialized for it. For example, multi-token words such as `multilingual' and `multilingualism' might both be included as token candidates for the in-boundary condition, even though the latter may also include additional attention patterns related to morphological or syntactic processing (e.g.\ subject--verb agreement, which has been shown to have distinct circuits \citealp{ferrando2024similarity}). As a result, targeted ablation may partially capture mechanisms unrelated to whole-word reconstruction.

The results suggest that this selection process introduces some noise. Random ablation has a comparatively larger effect in the English \texttt{qwen2.5} 7B model than in the other evaluated models, which may indicate either imperfect candidate selection or genuinely more distributed computation. As a post hoc sanity check, the 5\% random-ablation condition was rerun using a fixed ablation map across samples for the 7B model in English. This reduced performance by only 0.01, suggesting that some heads are not included in the targeted ablation which have a large impact on whole word reconstruction.

Also, ablation is an inherently interventional method and may induce non-typical model states. As noted by \citet{hase2021}, observed failures under ablation may reflect artifacts of the intervention rather than naturally occurring model behavior. While this study focuses on internal representations rather than downstream outputs, the possibility remains that the measured effects do not fully correspond to how the model operates under normal conditions.

Another limitation concerns the interpretation of attention head importance. As argued by \citet{wiegreffe2019}, the apparent importance of specific components may be explained by correlated or redundant structures elsewhere in the model. Consequently, the results do not establish a strictly causal role for individual attention heads, but rather indicate their importance within the constraints of the ablation setup.

\section{Implications \& Future Work}\label{sec:discussion_futurework}

The findings indicate that tokenizer design affects more than compression efficiency or vocabulary coverage. Differences in tokenization are associated with differences in how representations are structured and localized within the model. This has implications for interpretability research, as conclusions drawn from highly localized mechanisms may not transfer across tokenizer configurations or model families.

There are several paths of future work. This study does not consider the impact ablation has on generation. It could be the case that ablation does not hinder the model's ability to generate good next-token predictions. One direction of future work could take inspiration from \citet{basile2026} who managed to \textit{steer} output using semantic attention head ablation, but redesign the task for detokenization. Another unexplored area is how the phenomena emerges during training. \citet{korner2026} studied EuroLLM checkpoints to see how shared concept spaces emerged during training. This is unexplored for the area of detokenization.
