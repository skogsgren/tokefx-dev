\chapter{Discussion} \label{cha:discussion}

This chapter begins with a summary of the main findings in relation to the research questions. Section~\ref{sec:discussion_tokenizer} discusses the effects of tokenizer choice and Section~\ref{sec:discussion_qwen} on model size, followed by a reflection on limitations and implications in Section~\ref{sec:discussion_limitations}, and directions for future work in Section~\ref{sec:discussion_futurework}.

\begin{description}
\item[\textbf{RQ1}] The results show targeted ablation has a disproportionately large impact on word retrieval compared to random ablation. In most cases, models are unable to recover retrieval performance after targeted ablation, and the retrieval peak is consistently delayed. In extreme cases, such as the \texttt{Qwen2.5} 7B model, retrieval collapses from 0.79 to 0.01, while random ablation produces only minor changes. This pattern is consistent with findings by \citet{voita2019}, indicating that a small subset of attention heads plays a specialized and critical role. The early peak in retrieval is consistent with findings from \citet{kaplan2025}.

\item[\textbf{RQ2}] Tokenizer choice has impact on the formation of intermediary representations. Differences in retrieval performance cannot be explained by compression efficiency alone. For example, the \texttt{XGLM} tokenizer achieves substantially higher retrieval than \texttt{gpt2} in English (0.81 vs.\ 0.47), despite \texttt{gpt2} being better at compressing English. This suggests that tokenizer design has an impact on how the representations are structured within the model during training. At the same time, robustness to ablation does not follow a consistent pattern across tokenizers, and varies depending on language.

\item[\textbf{RQ3}] Larger models achieve higher retrieval scores, with the 7B model performing best overall and the 0.5B model performing worst. Mid-sized models (1.5B and 3B) show comparable performance, with variations across languages. Larger models show different retrieve rate patterns, with a distinct peak at early layers which is not present in the 1.5B and 3B models, that instead show a more distributed retrieval pattern. This suggests that scaling leads to more efficient but less distributed representations as models get bigger.

\end{description}

\section{Tokenizer Impact}\label{sec:discussion_tokenizer}

The impact of tokenizer choice can be partly explained by differences in compression and the resulting training dataset size in \texttt{Toksuite} models. Because all models are trained on a fixed number of tokens, tokenizers with lower compression rates expose the model to less raw text. As shown in Table~\ref{tab:fertility}, \texttt{byt5} operates on 1 byte/token, meaning that it is effectively trained on less data than more compressive tokenizers such as \texttt{xglm} which gets $\sim$4 bytes/token. This reduced exposure may limit the model's ability to learn specialized attention patterns. Instead of relying on a small set of heads to reconstruct word-level representations, the model may develop more distributed mechanisms for combining subtoken information, reducing the impact of targeted ablation.

One such factor may be cross-lingual transfer. Prior work shows that monolingual BERT models can learn abstractions that generalize across languages \citep{artetxe2020} and that embedding alignment correlates with transfer \citep{deshpande2022}. The \texttt{gpt2}-based model may therefore benefit from patterns learned in English and Turkish when processing Chinese, helping organize fragmented token sequences. However, explicitly multilingual tokenizers such as \texttt{aya} perform worse than \texttt{gpt2} in both English and Chinese despite more efficient tokenization, suggesting interactionary transfer effects consistent with the ``curse of multilinguality'' \citep{chang2024}.

Tokenizer efficiency may also influence how representations are distributed. Poor tokenization may force the model to distribute calculation across more components. In contrast, better tokenization may concentrate this functionality in a smaller set of specialized heads. This provides a plausible explanation for robustness differences: distributed representations are less sensitive to targeted ablation, while specialized ones degrade more sharply. This interpretation is supported by attention heatmaps for Chinese (Figure~\ref{fig:gpt2_heatmaps}), which show weaker specialization than in English and Turkish. Byte-level artifacts such as the ``ghost token'' effect \citep{jang2025} may further disrupt representation formation, particularly in languages where such artifacts are frequent, aligning with prior findings on the importance of pre-tokenization \citep{wegmann2025, arnett2025}.

Tokenizer architecture itself may also play a role. The \texttt{xglm} tokenizer achieves the highest retrieval performance in English, outperforming even larger models such as \texttt{qwen2.5-7B}, despite slightly worse compression than \texttt{gpt2}. This suggests that differences in tokenization strategy (e.g., Unigram vs.\ BPE) may influence how effectively embeddings and intermediate representations are learned. While prior work emphasizes the dominant role of the embedding layer as compared to the tokenizer itself\citep{wu2023}, these results indicate that tokenizer design can still shape how efficiently embeddings are learned during training. However, given that only one Unigram-based model is included, this remains speculative.

\section{Parameter Size Impact}\label{sec:discussion_qwen}

Retrieval performance generally increases with parameter size, with the 0.5B model performing worst and the 7B model performing best within the \texttt{qwen2.5} family. This is consistent with prior findings that embeddings of high-frequency words converge quickly during training, while low-frequency words take longer and retain alignment with the bias in their random initializations \citep{papadimitriou2025}.

However, this relationship does not strictly hold in our results. The 1.5B model slightly outperforms the 3B model in English and Chinese, despite similar overall retrieval levels. This suggests that increasing parameter size does not uniformly improve representation quality across configurations. One interpretation is that scaling changes \textit{how} representations are constructed within the model. Smaller models appear to rely on more distributed mechanisms, while larger models develop specialized components.

This shift is reflected in the layerwise retrieval patterns (Figure~\ref{fig:qwen_rr}). The 7B and 3B models exhibit distinct early peaks (within the first 25\% of layers), followed by a rapid decline, indicating that key representations are formed early and handled by specific components. In contrast, the 0.5B and 1.5B models show more gradual and distributed accumulation of retrieval across layers. Similar patterns have been observed in larger models \citep{kaplan2025}, as well as in studies showing that structured behaviors such as induction are less clearly localized in smaller models \citep{feucht2025}.

Within this framework, the 1.5B model may represent an intermediate regime in which layer specialization is emerging but not yet fully established. This could explain its performance relative to the 3B model despite more distributed behavior across layers, and is consistent with phase transition phenomena observed during training, where models abruptly shift toward more specialized structure after sufficient optimization \citep{elhage2022b,power2022}. Despite this, no clear difference in robustness to ablation is observed across model sizes. One possible explanation is that the ablation setup is too coarse to capture such differences, as removing a large number of heads may obscure more localized effects. This remains speculative and would require further study.

\section{Implications \& Future Work}\label{sec:discussion_futurework}

The results suggest that representation structure in transformer models is shaped not only by architecture and scale, but also by tokenizer design, with consequences for both research and practice. The degree of functional specialization appears to vary with tokenization, implying that interpretability findings based on localized components may not generalize across model configurations. For model development, the results suggest tokenizer choice influences not just the efficiency of learning embeddings, but influences how information is distributed within the model.

There are several paths of future work. This study does not consider the impact ablation has on generation. It could be the case that ablation does not hinder the model's ability to generate good next-token predictions. One potential future work could take inspiration from \citet{basile2026} who managed to \textit{steer} output using semantic attention head ablation, but redesign the task for detokenization. Another unexplored area is how the phenomena emerges during training. \citet{korner2026} studied EuroLLM checkpoints to see how shared concept spaces emerged during training. This is unexplored for the area of detokenization.

\section{Limitations}\label{sec:discussion_limitations}

Ablation is an inherently interventional method and may induce non-typical model states. As noted by \citet{hase2021}, observed failures under ablation may reflect artifacts of the intervention rather than naturally occurring behavior. While this study focuses on internal representations rather than downstream outputs, the possibility remains that the measured effects do not fully correspond to how the model operates under normal conditions.

Another limitation concerns the interpretation of attention head importance. As argued by \citet{wiegreffe2019}, the apparent importance of specific components may be explained by correlated or redundant structures elsewhere in the model. Consequently, the results do not establish a strictly causal role for individual attention heads, but rather indicate their importance within the constraints of the ablation setup.

Finally, the ablation procedure operates at a relatively coarse granularity (5\%). This may obscure more fine-grained differences in sensitivity between models, particularly when comparing across scales or tokenization strategies.
