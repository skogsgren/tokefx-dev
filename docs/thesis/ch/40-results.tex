\chapter{Results} \label{cha:results}

The \textbf{Results} chapter presents the outcomes of your study in a clear and objective manner. This is where you \textit{answer your research question(s)} using the data you have analyzed, but without interpreting or discussing implications—those are reserved for the next chapter.

\subsection*{Purpose of the Chapter}

The goal of this chapter is to:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Present the results of your analysis as directly and transparently as possible
  \item Let the reader assess what the data shows, without being influenced by your interpretation
  \item Use visual and tabular formats to enhance clarity and comparability
\end{itemize}

All results that are relevant to your research questions should be reported—regardless of whether they align with your expectations.

\subsection*{Structure and Content}

Structure the results logically, preferably in the same order as the research questions were introduced in earlier chapters. A suggested structure includes:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Overview of the Section} \\
  Begin with a short paragraph that outlines what will be covered in the results chapter, and how it is organized (e.g., by research question, by experiment, by dataset).

  \item \textbf{Results by Question or Theme} \\
  Present results in separate subsections for each research question or theme. For example:
  \begin{itemize}
    \item \textit{RQ1: Performance of multilingual models on low-resource languages}
    \item \textit{RQ2: Impact of domain adaptation}
  \end{itemize}
  Use informative headings for each subsection to guide the reader.

  \item \textbf{Figures and Tables} \\
  Use tables and figures to present quantitative data, model evaluations, comparisons with baselines, error rates, distributions, or qualitative categories. Follow these conventions:
  \begin{itemize}
    \item Tables and figures must be numbered and have descriptive captions.
    \item Always refer to them in the text (e.g., ``Table~\ref{tab:results1} shows...’’).
    \item Keep them clean and readable—avoid unnecessary precision or clutter.
  \end{itemize}

  \item \textbf{Textual Summary} \\
  Provide a brief but precise explanation of what each table or figure shows. Avoid interpretation here—stick to what the data says.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{Quantitative vs. Qualitative Results}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Quantitative Studies:} Report evaluation metrics such as accuracy, precision, recall, F1-score, BLEU, UAS/LAS, or confusion matrices. Include baseline comparisons and statistical significance tests if appropriate.

  \item \textbf{Qualitative Studies:} Summarize patterns, categories, or thematic structures found in your data. Quote examples where relevant. For typological or annotation studies, tables of linguistic patterns or frequency counts are useful.

  \item \textbf{Mixed Methods:} If you used both qualitative and quantitative approaches (e.g., error analysis + evaluation scores), present each separately, but highlight complementary findings.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Best Practices}

\begin{itemize}
  \item Present results in the same sequence as your research questions or hypotheses.
  \item Be transparent—include unexpected or negative results.
  \item Label all figures and tables consistently, e.g., ``Figure~\ref{fig:method_pipeline}'' or ``Table~\ref{tab:results1}''. The \texttt{fancyref} \LaTeX package can help with this: \Fref{fig:method_pipeline} and \Fref{tab:results1}.
  \item Avoid interpretation—save that for the Discussion chapter.
\end{itemize}
