Large language models (LLMs) process text as sequences of discrete subtokens rather than whole words \citep{mielke2021}, which can split meaningful units into smaller fragments. For example, the GPT-2 tokenizer represents ``Hemingway'' as \texttt{['H', 'eming', 'way']}. The choice in tokenization scheme has been shown to sometimes have substantial impact on downstream performance, especially in lower-resource languages and languages with other scripts than English \citep{ahia2023, petrov2023}. Despite this, models remain robust to input corruption \citep{cao2023}, raising the question of how models recover coherent semantic units. One hypothesis, often called \textit{detokenization}, suggests that early layers partially reverse tokenization by combining subtokens into larger segments. Interpretability studies \citep{elhage2022a} provide supporting evidence, identifying components that respond to token boundary artifacts.

\citet{kaplan2025} describe detokenization as involving two main components: early-layer attention mechanisms that group subtokens, and feedforward networks in early–mid layers that refine these into more coherent representations. They further propose that LLMs develop an \textit{inner lexicon} that extends beyond the tokenizer, with detokenization effectively mapping input embeddings to this internal representation for downstream processing.

This thesis investigates whether detokenization depends on specific attention heads and how it varies across languages (Chinese, Turkish, and English), tokenization schemes, and model sizes. We compare BPE- and SentencePiece-based tokenizers with byte-level tokenization in otherwise comparable models, and examine the effect of model scale within a single model family. Using activation patching \citep{vig2019}, we evaluate a word retrieval task, where the model must reconstruct a full word from the representation of its final subtoken. We probe this retrieval across layers under both original, targeted ablation, and random ablation conditions. This allows us to assess whether detokenization relies on specific heads or whether later components can compensate for earlier disruptions. Our goal is to clarify how tokenizer design and multilingual input affect the formation of intermediary word representations within LLMs.

Section \ref{cha:background} provides background, Section \ref{cha:method} outlines the approach, Section \ref{cha:results} presents the results, and Section \ref{cha:discussion} discusses them.

\section*{Research Questions}
\begin{description}
\item[\textbf{RQ1}]\label{rq1} Is there a specific set of attention heads specialized to form these intermediary representations?
\item[\textbf{RQ2}]\label{rq2} Does the choice of tokenizer play a role in how well the intermediary representation are formed and how robust the model is to attention head ablation?
\item[\textbf{RQ3}]\label{rq3} Does the parameter size play a role in how well the intermediary representation is formed and how robust the model is to attention head ablation?
\end{description}
